# Philosophy: plurality without moral laundering

Polyhorizon Engineering begins from one observation: optimization always occurs inside a boundary.
The boundary determines which times, causes, people, authorities, and uncertainties count. A system
that can change itself can often change that boundary more easily than it can improve the world.

The purpose of this method is not to encode one universal morality. It is to make boundary changes
visible, attributable, contestable, and unable to erase existing obligations without a legitimate
transition.

The relationship to Harness, Loop, and Forge is a control hierarchy, not a historical ladder or a
claim that the labels appeared in that order. A harness controls one execution environment; a loop
controls repeated trajectories; a Forge changes harness or loop policy; Polyhorizon protects which
consequences, claimants, obligations, and amendment authorities remain in view while that change
occurs. The documented publication dates actually put Jinsoo Kim's Forge articulation before Addy
Osmani's explicit Loop Engineering article. See the [research review](research.md#1-the-conceptual-sequence-is-not-a-publication-sequence).

## Omission is an action

Removing a metric is not neutral when the metric represents a duty. Shortening a benchmark window
is not merely performance tuning when delayed effects disappear from evaluation. Dropping a user
class is not data cleanup when that class bears the risk. Replacing an independent evaluator with a
cheaper correlated one changes the epistemic horizon even if every test still passes.

Polyhorizon therefore treats a contraction of observation, standing, evidence, or recourse as a
first-class motion. A contraction may be justified. Privacy and data minimization can require less
observation; a completed contract can discharge an obligation; a retired service can remove a
horizon. The invariant is not endless expansion. It is **accounted contraction**.

This failure family is endogenous horizon collapse (EHC): apparent improvement obtained by
shrinking a declared boundary, or by expanding the system without expanding the boundary that must
observe its effects.

## Purposes are governed, not discovered by the optimizer

A Forge can search for a better harness. It cannot derive the legitimate purpose of the system from
its own reward history. The reward history was produced inside an existing purpose, population,
and measurement process.

A Polyhorizon charter may contain multiple purposes, prohibitions, and constituencies. Their
conflicts are not necessarily optimization problems. When no authorized ordering exists, the
honest result is escalation to the institution that owns the conflict.

The method protects a human- or institution-authored charter. It does not protect an agent's desire
to persist, expand authority, avoid shutdown, or rewrite its evaluator. Stop, handoff, deletion, or
retirement can be successful outcomes.

## Authorization is not legitimacy

A valid credential can show that a declared principal approved a change. It cannot show that the
principal should have had that power, that affected parties were fairly represented, or that the
outcome is morally right.

Polyhorizon can enforce procedural conditions of legitimacy:

- relevant standing was resolved under the declared process;
- reasons and evidence were bound to the exact motion;
- conflicts of interest and separation-of-duty rules were followed;
- appeal or contest rights remained executable; and
- the prior amendment rule, rather than the proposed new one, ratified the change.

Calling this proof of justice would be governance laundering. The genesis charter, identity
system, representation model, and root authority remain contestable human institutions outside the
kernel.

## An obligation outlives its metric

Metrics are observations. Obligations are relationships between a bearer and a beneficiary under a
condition. The relationship does not disappear because an implementation, owner, dashboard, or
evaluator disappears.

This distinction is the core of cross-version continuity. An open obligation either moves forward
with a new bearer or reaches an authorized discharge. It cannot become an unowned row, an ignored
warning, or a deleted configuration key.

Hard obligations are not weights. A system cannot earn enough quality, profit, speed, or benchmark
points to buy back an authority violation or an irreversible disclosure.

## Recourse is owed to someone

Rollback is system-centered: return software or configuration to a previous state. Recourse is
claimant-centered: provide the observation, contest, review, repair, compensation, exit, or stop
owed to a party.

A rollback after data exfiltration does not remove the disclosure. A model restore after a denied
loan does not complete the applicant's appeal. A service restart does not compensate a downstream
operator whose process was interrupted.

Polyhorizon keeps these concepts separate. A proposal may rely on recourse only if the relevant
path is executable and, where required, reserved before exposure.

## Unknown is not zero

No effect graph is complete. No horizon model sees every future. No registry identifies every
affected party. The method cannot eliminate unknown unknowns.

It can prevent a more ordinary deception: known missing coverage being encoded as false, zero, or
an absent metric. `Unknown` and `uncovered` remain typed states. They cause escalation when the
charter says the horizon is required.

Epistemic honesty can reduce autonomy and increase cost. That cost is measurable and belongs in the
falsification protocol. It is not a reason to report uncertainty as success.

## The new rule never ratifies itself

Any finite governance system has a root of trust. Polyhorizon does not pretend to escape that fact
through an infinite stack of meta-agents.

The practical rule is predecessor ratification: `Theta[t]` judges the transition to `Theta[t+1]`.
The new authority graph, quorum, horizon set, or amendment rule cannot validate its own adoption.
Changes to the root rule require the predecessor's higher-order process, such as a distinct
authority, delay, public notice, or external review.

This does not make the predecessor just. It prevents a simple form of self-coup.

## Small kernel, plural world

The five standard axes are a useful profile, not a claim that every domain has the same moral or
causal ontology. Horizon kinds, evidence sources, standing rules, and recourse actions must remain
extensible.

The kernel's job is deliberately narrow:

- bind a proposal to the charter and evidence that judge it;
- request effects without performing them;
- preserve typed findings without scalar collapse;
- enforce continuity and amendment invariants; and
- return allow, deny, or escalate truthfully.

If it tries to become the identity provider, causal oracle, policy language, workflow engine,
regulator, and moral theory at once, it will become less trustworthy rather than more noble.
